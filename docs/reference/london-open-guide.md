# Accelerated London System: Development Guide

## 1. Purpose of the system

The Accelerated London begins with:

**1. d4**, followed whenever possible by **Bf4** before developing the kingside knight.

The opening is called “accelerated” because White develops the dark-squared bishop immediately instead of beginning with Nf3. This preserves move-order flexibility and ensures that the bishop reaches an active square before e3 closes the c1–h6 diagonal.

The London is a system, but it is not a sequence that should be played without looking at Black’s position. White normally aims for a familiar structure, but several Black setups require White to change:

* The order in which pieces are developed
* Whether the c-pawn goes to c3 or c4
* Whether the b1 knight goes to d2 or c3
* Whether White plays for e4 or Ne5
* Whether White castles kingside or queenside

The goal of this guide is to explain where every piece normally belongs, when it should move, and which conditions change the plan.

---

# 2. The default London structure

Against a quiet setup involving ...d5, ...Nf6, ...e6 and normal piece development, White generally wants:

* Pawn on d4
* Dark-squared bishop on f4
* Pawn on e3
* Pawn on c3
* Queenside knight on d2
* Kingside knight on f3
* Light-squared bishop on d3 or e2
* King castled kingside

A typical development sequence is:

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 e6 4.Nd2 Be7 5.Ngf3 O-O 6.Bd3 c5 7.c3**

The exact order is flexible. The important relationship is:

> In the traditional London structure, c3 and Nbd2 belong together.

White builds the pawn triangle c3–d4–e3, controls e5, supports the d4 pawn and prepares either Ne5 or e4.

---

# 3. Development priorities

Before choosing the next setup move, check the position in this order:

1. Is Black threatening something immediately?
2. Is Black attacking d4 with ...c5?
3. Is Black attacking b2 with ...Qb6?
4. Has Black developed the c8 bishop outside the pawn chain?
5. Is White building a c3/Nbd2 structure or a c4/Nc3 structure?
6. Is the intended central break e4, c4 or d5?
7. Can the king be castled safely?
8. Which piece is currently least active?

The London setup is the default. Concrete threats override the default.

---

# 4. The d-pawn: d2–d4

## Default move

**1.d4**

This is the foundation of the entire opening.

## Purpose

The pawn on d4:

* Claims central space
* Controls c5 and e5
* Opens the c1 bishop
* Opens the queen’s d1–a4 diagonal
* Provides the central anchor for the London structure

## When the pawn normally stays on d4

Against ...d5, White usually keeps the pawn on d4 while completing development.

The pawn is protected by:

* e3
* c3
* Nf3
* Sometimes Qb3, Qc2 or Nbd2

## When to advance d4–d5

Advance to **d5** when Black challenges the center with an immediate ...c5 before establishing a pawn on d5.

Examples:

**1.d4 c5 2.d5**

or:

**1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4 c5 3.d5**

The purpose is to gain space and enter a Benoni-type structure rather than allowing Black to exchange the c-pawn for White’s central pawn without consequence.

After d5, White normally follows with:

* Nc3
* e4
* Nf3
* Be2 or Bc4
* O-O

This is no longer a standard c3 London structure. White is playing a space-based central position.

## When to capture on c5

Consider **dxc5** when:

* Black has played ...c5 and recovering the pawn will cost Black time
* The capture prevents Black from quickly establishing control of e5
* White can develop with Nc3, Qd2 and possibly O-O-O
* Black’s queen will have to make several moves to regain the pawn
* The resulting open position favors White’s development

Do not capture automatically. Determine whether Black can regain the pawn while improving a piece.

## When to capture on e5

Capture on e5 when Black’s ...e5 break would otherwise establish a strong center, especially in positions where White can open the d-file.

In some King’s Indian or Pirc-style structures, dxe5 is used to open the d-file for a rook after White castles queenside.

---

# 5. The dark-squared bishop: c1–f4

## Default destination

**Bf4**

This is the defining move of the London System.

## Timing

Develop the bishop before playing e3.

The usual beginning is:

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4**

or:

**1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4**

## Purpose

From f4, the bishop:

* Controls e5
* Helps support a knight on e5
* Pressures c7
* Remains outside the e3–d4 pawn chain
* Can retreat while preserving pressure
* May participate in an attack against the kingside

## Why Bf4 is normally played before Nf3

Playing Bf4 first preserves several options:

* Nbd2 can be played before Nf3
* The queen retains access along d1–h5
* White can use f3 in aggressive structures
* White can respond more flexibly to ...c5 and ...Qb6
* White can sometimes use Be2 to attack a knight on h5

Nf3 is not inherently bad. The point is that it should not be played automatically when delaying it gives White a useful tactical or positional option.

## Retreat to g3

**Bg3** is the normal retreat after:

* ...Bd6
* ...Nh5
* Direct pressure against the bishop

White often keeps the tension rather than exchanging immediately.

After ...Bxg3, White may recapture with hxg3, opening the h-file for the rook.

## Retreat to h2

The bishop may retreat through g3 to h2 when:

* White has played h3
* Black is trying to exchange the bishop with ...Nh5
* White wants to preserve the bishop for a kingside attack

The bishop on h2 continues to support e5 and may later return to g3 or f4.

## Move to g5

**Bg5** is useful when:

* Black’s knight on f6 is an important defender
* Pinning the knight increases pressure against d5 or e5
* Black has played ...Nh5 and Bg5 preserves the bishop tactically
* White wants to provoke ...h6 or ...f6

Do not play Bg5 merely because the square is available. The bishop is usually more stable on f4 or g3.

## Move to h4

The bishop may reach h4 after Bg5 and ...h6 or ...f6.

From h4 it may:

* Preserve itself from attack
* Maintain a pin
* Target f6
* Support a kingside attack

Check for ...g5 before committing the bishop to h4.

## Exchange on d6

**Bxd6** is appropriate when:

* Black’s bishop is an important defender
* The exchange damages Black’s pawn structure
* White intends to play f4 and control e5
* Retaining the bishop allows Black to improve with tempo

Do not exchange automatically. In many positions, Bg3 preserves more tension.

## Exchange on b8

**Bxb8** is rarely positional. It is normally played only for a concrete tactical reason, such as removing the knight that could block a check or defend a critical square.

# 6. The e-pawn: e2–e3 or e4

## Default destination

**e3**

## Timing

Play e3 after the dark-squared bishop has left c1.

## Purpose

The move e3:

* Supports d4
* Opens the f1 bishop
* Builds the London pawn triangle
* Prepares Bd3 or Be2
* Supports a later e4 break
* Stabilizes White’s center

## When e3 should be played early

Play e3 early when:

* Black has played ...d5
* Black is challenging the center with ...c5
* White intends to build the traditional London structure
* The d4 pawn requires additional support

## When e3 may be delayed

Delay e3 when:

* Black’s unusual move order creates a tactical issue
* White is playing a Jobava-style Nc3 and e4 setup
* White is using g3 and Bg2
* White needs c3 first to prevent ...g5 tactics
* Black’s ...b6 and ...Ba6 plan makes castling timing important

## The e3–e4 break

In many London positions, e4 is White’s main freeing move.

Prepare e4 with some combination of:

* Nbd2
* Bd3
* Qe2 or Qc2
* Re1
* Nf3
* Control of e4 and d5

Play e4 when:

* Black’s center is stable but passive
* Black has committed pieces away from the center
* Black’s bishop has not yet reached b7
* Opening the center favors White’s better development
* Black has advanced ...c4 and White needs to challenge the pawn chain

Do not play e4 merely because it is thematic. Count the attackers and defenders and determine how White will recapture after ...dxe4.

## Direct e2–e4

White may play e4 in one move when:

* The b1 knight is already on c3
* Black has fianchettoed with ...g6
* Black has not established a strong pawn on d5
* White is deliberately entering a Pirc or King’s Indian structure

A common branch is:

**1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4**

This is not the traditional c3 London. White has converted the opening into a broad-center attacking system.

---

# 7. The c-pawn: c2–c3 or c4

This is the most important structural choice in the opening.

## Default destination: c3

Choose **c3** when White wants the traditional London structure.

## Purpose of c3

The move c3:

* Reinforces d4
* Builds the c3–d4–e3 pawn triangle
* Supports a later e4 break
* Restricts some ...Nb4 ideas
* Gives White a stable center
* Coordinates naturally with Nbd2

## Conditions favoring c3

Play c3 when:

* Black has a normal ...d5 and ...e6 structure
* White wants a solid center
* The b1 knight is going to d2
* White intends to play Ne5
* White plans a kingside attack
* Black has not created a reason to challenge d5 immediately

## Timing of c3

c3 may be played before or after Nbd2.

A useful accelerated order is:

**Bf4, e3, Nbd2, c3, Ngf3**

Playing Nbd2 before Nf3 preserves flexibility against ...c5 and ...Qb6.

## Alternative destination: c4

Choose **c4** when White wants to challenge Black’s d5 pawn directly.

The move often transforms the position into a London–Queen’s Gambit hybrid.

## Conditions favoring c4

Play or strongly consider c4 when:

* Black develops the c8 bishop early with ...Bf5
* Black has played ...c6 and ...d5, creating a Slav structure
* Black’s d5 pawn can be pressured immediately
* Qb3 will create pressure against both b7 and d5
* White intends to develop the b1 knight to c3
* White wants queenside play rather than a fixed London triangle

A typical idea is:

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 Bf5 4.c4**

followed by:

* Nc3
* Qb3
* Nf3
* Be2
* O-O

## Why ...Bf5 encourages c4

When Black plays ...Bf5 before ...e6:

* The bishop no longer helps defend b7
* Black has committed a tempo to the bishop
* White can attack d5 with c4
* Qb3 may attack d5 and b7 simultaneously
* Black may be forced into passive queen or queenside moves

The rule is not “play c4 whenever Black moves a bishop.” The important condition is that Black’s c8 bishop has left the queenside, especially to f5.

## Relationship between the c-pawn and the b1 knight

Use this as the default rule:

* **c3 usually means Nbd2**
* **c4 usually means Nc3**
* **Nc3 prevents c3**
* **Nbd2 keeps both c3 and c4 available**

This decision should be made before White places the b1 knight.

---

# 8. The queenside knight: b1–d2 or c3

## Default destination: d2

In the traditional London, develop the knight to **d2**.

## Purpose of Nbd2

The knight on d2:

* Supports e4
* Reinforces f3
* Helps control e5
* Leaves the c-pawn free to move
* Supports recaptures on e4
* Can reroute through f1, b3 or f3

## Why Nbd2 may come before Nf3

Against setups involving ...c5, ...Nc6 and ...Qb6, Nbd2 can be useful before Nf3 because:

* Rb1 may become available without blocking development
* The queen retains the d1–h5 diagonal
* White can meet ...Qb6 with Qb3 or Rb1 depending on the structure
* White avoids committing the kingside knight before the center is clarified

This is a move-order tool, not a universal prohibition against Nf3.

## Alternative destination: c3

Develop the knight to **c3** when:

* White has played or intends to play c4
* White wants to play e4 in one move
* Black has fianchettoed with ...g6
* White is entering a Jobava-style structure
* Nb5 creates a real threat against c7
* Black’s queen on b6 can be met with Rb1 and active play

## The Nb5 idea

After Nc3 and Bf4, **Nb5** may threaten Nxc7+.

Use Nb5 when:

* Black cannot answer comfortably with ...a6
* Black’s only satisfactory defense is ...Na6
* The knight on b5 cannot be driven away profitably
* White gains time by forcing Black into an awkward defensive setup

Do not play Nb5 automatically. Check:

* ...Na6
* ...Bb4+
* ...Bd6
* ...a6
* Queen checks
* Whether the knight can safely return

## Reroutes from d2

The d2 knight may later move to:

* f3, reinforcing e5
* b3, attacking c5
* f1–g3, supporting a kingside attack
* e4, occupying the center
* c4, attacking queenside weaknesses

---

# 9. The kingside knight: g1–f3

## Default destination

**Nf3**

## Purpose

The knight on f3:

* Defends d4
* Controls e5
* Supports Ne5
* Prepares castling
* Helps prepare e4
* Defends the kingside

## When to develop it normally

Play Nf3 early when:

* Black is not creating pressure with ...c5 and ...Qb6
* White has already determined the c-pawn structure
* Castling is the immediate priority
* Black cannot exploit the move order

## When to delay it

Delay Nf3 when:

* White wants Nbd2 first
* Black may play ...c5, ...Nc6 and ...Qb6
* White wants to preserve the d1–h5 queen diagonal
* White may need f3
* White may use Be2 against a knight on h5
* White has not yet chosen between c3 and c4

## The Ne5 outpost

A knight on e5 is one of the central London ideas.

Play Ne5 when:

* The knight is supported by d4, f4 or another piece
* Black cannot exchange it under favorable circumstances
* The knight interferes with Black’s development
* White intends to attack the kingside
* The move helps prevent ...e5
* Black’s light-squared bishop remains trapped

## Supporting Ne5 with f4

White may play f4 to reinforce a knight on e5.

This creates a Stonewall-like structure:

* c3
* d4
* e3
* f4

Use f4 when:

* The center is stable
* White has committed to a kingside attack
* Black cannot immediately break with ...e5
* White’s king is safe
* The dark squares around White’s king remain manageable

Do not play f4 merely to defend a knight if it creates tactical weaknesses.

## Rerouting the knight

The f3 knight may later move to:

* e5, the main outpost
* g5, joining an attack
* h4, attacking f5 or g6
* d2, if the structure requires regrouping
* e1–d3, in closed positions

---

# 10. The light-squared bishop: f1–d3, e2, b5 or g2

## Default destination: d3

The most active traditional square is **d3**.

## Purpose of Bd3

From d3, the bishop:

* Targets h7
* Supports e4
* Coordinates with Qc2 or Qe2
* Supports a Greek Gift sacrifice on h7 in suitable positions
* Helps create a kingside attack

## When Bd3 is best

Choose Bd3 when:

* Black has castled kingside
* The h7 square may become weak
* White plans Ne5 and f4
* White plans e4
* Black cannot gain time with ...Nb4

## Alternative destination: e2

Choose **Be2** when:

* The bishop would be exposed on d3
* Black can play ...Nb4
* White is facing a ...g6 setup
* White wants to support h5
* White wants a safe route to kingside castling
* White may later play Bf3
* The d3–h7 diagonal has little value

Against fianchetto setups, Be2 is often more useful than Bd3 because the h7 square is protected differently and the bishop can support a kingside pawn advance.

## Alternative destination: b5

Choose **Bb5** when:

* Pinning the c6 knight weakens Black’s control of e5
* Black’s knight is an important central defender
* White may exchange on c6 and damage Black’s structure
* The pin helps White carry out e4 or Ne5

## Alternative destination: g2

Choose **Bg2** only when White has deliberately played g3 and entered a Buckingham-style setup.

The plan becomes:

* g3
* Bg2
* Nf3
* O-O
* Nbd2
* e3
* c3

This is a separate version of the London. Do not combine g3 with the ordinary Bd3 plan without a clear reason.

---

# 11. The queen

The queen has no single mandatory development square.

Its placement depends on White’s intended pawn break and Black’s pressure.

## Qc2

Play **Qc2** when:

* Supporting e4
* Creating a battery with Bd3 against h7
* Defending b2
* Meeting pressure from ...Qb6
* Preparing kingside play

Be alert to ...Bf5, which may attack the queen with tempo.

## Qe2

Play **Qe2** when:

* Supporting e4
* Supporting Bd3
* Connecting the rooks
* Preparing O-O-O in some positions
* Preventing or answering ...Ba6
* Supporting a kingside attack

## Qb3

Play **Qb3** when:

* Black has played ...Bf5
* b7 is weak
* White has played c4
* d5 is under pressure
* Black’s queen is on b6 and exchanging queens is acceptable

Do not play Qb3 automatically. Determine whether ...c4 will force the queen to lose time.

## Qc1

Play **Qc1** when:

* Black’s queen attacks b2 from b6
* Qb3 is tactically undesirable
* The queen must defend b2 while avoiding harassment
* White intends to attack later with Qc2, Qd1 or another reroute

Qc1 is passive but may be the correct temporary square.

## Qd2

Play **Qd2** when:

* White intends to castle queenside
* White plans Bh6 against a fianchetto
* White is supporting e4
* White is entering a Pirc or King’s Indian attacking structure
* White intends to play f3, g4 and h4

## Qa4

Qa4 or Qa4+ can be useful when:

* The queen gains time by checking
* White can pressure c6 or a6
* Black has weakened the queenside
* The move supports tactical play after an exchange on c6

It is a tactical square, not a standard development square.

---

# 12. The king

## Default plan: castle kingside

White normally castles kingside after:

* Nf3
* Bd3 or Be2
* The center is stable

Kingside castling is the safest and most common choice in the traditional London.

## When to castle quickly

Castle promptly when:

* The center may open
* Black has completed development
* White has no concrete reason to delay
* White intends to play Re1 and e4

## When to delay castling

Delay castling when:

* White is deciding between kingside and queenside castling
* The rook may be useful on g1
* The center is closed and White has an immediate tactical opportunity
* Black has committed to one wing and White may castle on the other

## Queenside castling

Castle queenside when White has deliberately adopted an aggressive setup involving:

* Nc3
* e4
* Qd2
* Sometimes f3
* A kingside pawn storm with g4 and h4

This is common against:

* King’s Indian structures
* Pirc structures
* Modern Defense structures
* Some Jobava lines

Do not castle queenside while mechanically building the ordinary c3 London. Long castling requires a different piece arrangement and a concrete attacking plan.

---

# 13. The rooks

## The f1 rook

After kingside castling, the f1 rook often goes to e1.

### Re1

Play Re1 when:

* Preparing e4
* The e-file may open
* Supporting a knight or pawn on e5
* Increasing central pressure

## The a1 rook

The a1 rook may go to:

* c1 in c4 structures
* b1 against queen pressure on b2
* e1 when the other rook has another role
* d1 when the d-file opens

### Rb1

Play Rb1 when:

* Black’s queen is on b6 or b2
* White has developed the b1 knight to c3
* The b-pawn is under pressure
* The rook can gain time against Black’s queen

### Rc1

Play Rc1 when:

* White has played c4
* The c-file is open or likely to open
* White is pressuring c7
* Queenside play is the main plan

### Rd1

Play Rd1 when:

* The d-file opens
* White has exchanged on e5 or c5
* Black has an isolated or backward d-pawn
* White has castled queenside and the rook already occupies d1

### Rg1

Play Rg1 when:

* The g-file has opened
* White has recaptured on f4 with the g-pawn
* White has delayed castling
* White is launching a direct kingside attack

A rook should move to a file because the file will open or because it supports a pawn break. Do not place rooks on thematic squares without a plan.

---

# 14. Supporting pawn moves

## The h-pawn

### h3

Play h3 when:

* Creating a retreat route for the f4 bishop through g3 to h2
* Preventing ...Bg4
* Preparing Bh2
* Giving the king luft
* Preparing g4 under controlled conditions

Do not play h3 before urgent development unless it solves a specific problem.

### h4

Play h4 when:

* Black has fianchettoed
* White plans h5
* The bishop on f4 needs protection against a trapping plan
* White has castled queenside
* White has enough central control to begin a wing attack

## The f-pawn

### f4

Play f4 when:

* Supporting Ne5
* Preventing ...e5
* Beginning a kingside attack
* Entering a Stonewall-like structure

### f3

Play f3 when:

* Supporting e4
* Chasing a bishop from g4
* Preparing g4 in a Jobava or queenside-castling structure
* The g1 knight has not been committed to f3

f3 weakens the king and should normally be connected to queenside castling or a closed center.

## The a-pawn

Play a3 when:

* Preventing ...Nb4
* Preparing b4
* Preventing a bishop from pinning or capturing on b4
* Supporting queenside expansion

Play a4 when:

* Restricting ...b5
* Chasing a queen on b6
* Fixing queenside weaknesses
* Supporting a5

## The b-pawn

Play b3 when:

* Challenging a pawn on c4
* Developing the bishop to b2 in unusual structures
* Supporting c4
* Stabilizing queenside light squares

Play b4 when:

* White has space on the queenside
* Black has castled queenside
* White has a c4 structure
* The move gains space without exposing the king

# 15. Development against Black’s main setups

## A. Black plays ...d5, ...Nf6 and ...e6

Use the traditional London structure:

* d4
* Bf4
* e3
* Nbd2
* c3
* Ngf3
* Bd3 or Be2
* O-O

Main plans:

* Ne5
* f4
* Qf3 or Qe2
* e4
* Re1
* Kingside attack

A model sequence is:

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 e6 4.Nd2 Be7 5.Ngf3 O-O 6.Bd3 c5 7.c3**

---

## B. Black plays an early ...c5 after ...d5

Example:

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 c5**

White should first stabilize or clarify the center.

A solid response is:

**3.e3**

Then choose between:

* c3 and Nbd2
* Nc3 with active piece play
* dxc5 in selected positions

If Black follows with ...Nc6 and ...Qb6, avoid developing on autopilot. White may need:

* Nbd2
* Qb3
* Rb1
* Nc3
* c4
* dxc5

The exact choice depends on whether the c-pawn has already moved and whether White is willing to sacrifice b2 for development.

---

## C. Black plays ...Bf5 early

Challenge the center with:

* c4
* Nc3
* Qb3

A model structure is:

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 Bf5 4.c4 e6 5.Nc3**

followed by Qb3.

White’s play is directed against:

* d5
* b7
* Black’s queenside development

Do not build c3 automatically once Black has made d5 and b7 vulnerable.

---

## D. Black plays ...g6

White has two main approaches.

### Traditional approach

Use:

* e3
* Nf3
* Be2
* O-O
* c3
* Nbd2

This is safe but allows Black a normal King’s Indian or Grünfeld-style position.

### Active approach

Use:

* Nc3
* e4
* Qd2
* O-O-O
* h4 and g4

A model beginning is:

**1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4**

This is the more ambitious repertoire branch.

Against a fianchetto, the light-squared bishop often belongs on e2 rather than d3.

---

## E. Black plays an immediate ...c5

Example:

**1.d4 c5**

Play:

**2.d5**

White then normally develops with:

* Nc3
* e4
* Nf3
* Be2 or Bc4
* O-O

Do not attempt to force the standard c3 London structure when Black has already created a Benoni position.

---

## F. Black plays ...c6 before ...d5

This signals a Slav or Caro-Kann structure.

White may choose:

### London approach

* Bf4
* e3
* c3
* Nbd2
* Ngf3
* Bd3
* O-O

### Active repertoire approach

* Nc3
* f3
* e4

The second approach attempts to exploit Black’s early ...c6 by building a large center. It is sharper and should be treated as a separate branch rather than part of the default London setup.

---

## G. Black plays ...b6

Black intends ...Bb7 and may enter a Queen’s Indian structure.

Consider:

* Nc3
* e4
* Bd3
* Nf3
* Qe2
* O-O or O-O-O

If Black combines ...b6 with ...Bb4, White may use:

* e3
* Bd3
* Ne2
* O-O
* a3

The knight often belongs on c3 in these positions because White wants e4 and active central control.

Be alert to ...Ba6. Qe2 may preserve the light-squared bishop and castling rights.

---

## H. Black plays ...f5

Against the Dutch, White should fight for e4.

A practical setup is:

* Nc3
* Bg5
* e4
* Nf3
* Bd3
* O-O

The immediate idea is:

**1.d4 f5 2.Nc3**

followed by Bg5 and e4.

This is more direct than forcing a normal London structure against Black’s kingside expansion.

---

## I. Black plays ...e5: the Englund Gambit

Accept the pawn:

**1.d4 e5 2.dxe5**

Then develop accurately against Black’s queen activity:

* Nf3
* Bf4
* Bd2
* Nc3
* Rb1 when necessary

Do not ignore Black’s concrete threats in order to complete a London setup. The Englund is a tactical opening and should be met tactically.

---

# 16. Default development orders

## Quiet classical setup

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 e6 4.Nd2 Be7 5.Ngf3 O-O 6.Bd3 c5 7.c3**

## Anti-London with ...c5

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 c5 3.e3 Nc6 4.c3 Qb6 5.Nd2**

The queen response depends on Black’s exact move order.

## Early ...Bf5

**1.d4 d5 2.Bf4 Nf6 3.e3 Bf5 4.c4 e6 5.Nc3**

followed by Qb3.

## Fianchetto setup

**1.d4 Nf6 2.Bf4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Qd2**

followed by O-O-O and kingside expansion.

## Immediate Benoni

**1.d4 c5 2.d5 Nf6 3.Nc3 d6 4.e4**

followed by Nf3, Be2 and O-O.

# 17. Common move-order mistakes

## Playing every setup move automatically

The London is not complete merely because White has played Bf4, e3, c3, Nf3 and Bd3. The moves must respond to Black’s setup.

## Committing the b1 knight too early

Nc3 and Nbd2 lead to different structures.

Decide first:

* Does the c-pawn belong on c3?
* Does White need e4 immediately?
* Is Nb5 useful?
* Is c4 the correct central break?

## Playing Nf3 without considering ...c5 and ...Qb6

Nf3 is a normal move, but delaying it may preserve:

* Nbd2 first
* f3
* Qh5 ideas
* Be2 against ...Nh5
* Greater flexibility against queen pressure

## Playing c3 when c4 is required

If Black has played ...Bf5 and made d5 and b7 vulnerable, c3 may be too passive. Challenge the center with c4.

## Playing c4 without coordinating the knight

After c4, the b1 knight usually belongs on c3. Do not play Nbd2 and c4 automatically unless there is a specific reason.

## Playing Ne5 without support

Before Ne5, calculate:

* Can Black play Nxe5?
* Which pawn recaptures?
* Can Black play f6?
* Is d4 left weak?
* Does the knight actually improve White’s position?

## Attacking before castling or controlling the center

Pawn attacks with h4, g4 and f4 are strongest when:

* The center is closed
* White’s king is safe
* White has enough developed pieces
* Black has committed the king

# 18. Final decision rules

Use these rules as a compact guide during a game.

1. Begin with d4.
2. Develop the dark-squared bishop to f4 before e3.
3. Against a quiet ...d5 and ...e6 structure, build c3–d4–e3.
4. In the c3 structure, develop the b1 knight to d2.
5. Consider Nbd2 before Nf3 when Black may use ...c5 and ...Qb6.
6. Against an early ...Bf5, challenge d5 with c4 and pressure b7 with Qb3.
7. In a c4 structure, develop the b1 knight to c3.
8. Against ...g6, consider Nc3 and e4 rather than a passive setup.
9. Against an immediate ...c5, advance d5 and accept a Benoni structure.
10. Place the light-squared bishop on d3 when attacking h7; place it on e2 when d3 has no purpose or Black has fianchettoed.
11. Use Ne5 only when it is supported and cannot be removed favorably.
12. Prepare e4 with pieces; do not treat it as an automatic pawn push.
13. Castle kingside in the traditional London.
14. Castle queenside only after deliberately choosing Qd2, Nc3 and an attacking setup.
15. Move the queen and rooks according to the intended pawn break and open files.
16. Complete development before beginning a wing attack unless a concrete tactic justifies acting immediately.

# 19. The central organizing principle

Most Accelerated London positions can be understood by identifying which of these three structures White is entering:

## Structure One: Traditional London

* d4
* Bf4
* e3
* c3
* Nbd2
* Nf3
* Bd3
* O-O

Main plans:

* Ne5
* f4
* e4
* Kingside attack

## Structure Two: Active c4 London

* d4
* Bf4
* e3
* c4
* Nc3
* Nf3
* Qb3
* Be2 or Bd3
* O-O

Main plans:

* Pressure d5
* Pressure b7
* Open the c-file
* Queenside play

## Structure Three: Nc3 and e4 attack

* d4
* Bf4
* Nc3
* e4
* Qd2
* Nf3 or f3
* O-O-O
* h4 and g4

Main plans:

* Broad central control
* Remove Black’s fianchettoed bishop
* Open the h- or g-file
* Attack the kingside

The correct development order becomes much easier once White identifies which of these three structures fits Black’s position.
